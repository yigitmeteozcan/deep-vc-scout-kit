## Example 1

**Company:**
IndustrAI Hub (fictional)

**Why rejected:**
Horizontal AI platform claiming to serve "manufacturing, logistics, energy, and construction" simultaneously. No specific buyer workflow defined. Founding team has a background in consumer SaaS and data analytics — no prior industrial operations experience. Only evidence of traction is a vague "10 enterprise conversations" with no named customers or LOIs. Found on TechCrunch and Crunchbase before the fund saw them.

**Red flag:**
"AI for enterprise operations" with no specific workflow — the classic buzzword-first description that hides lack of product focus.

**Pattern to avoid:**
Any company describing its market as multiple industrial sectors simultaneously without a specific entry workflow. If a company cannot name the exact job title of its buyer and the exact spreadsheet or paper process it replaces, it is not a focused industrial SaaS play.

## Example 2

**Company:**
GreenRoute SaaS (fictional)

**Why rejected:**
Logistics route optimization tool that has been operating since 2019 with no visible growth signal since 2021. Website has not been updated. CEO LinkedIn last active 18 months ago. Originally interesting concept but appears to have stalled. No recent grant, customer announcement, or team update found.

**Red flag:**
5+ year old company with no update in 18+ months. Website and founder LinkedIn both inactive. Classic case of a company that has either quietly shut down or is running on fumes without growth.

**Pattern to avoid:**
Companies older than 3-4 years with no recent signal (launch, hire, grant, customer, funding) in the last 12-18 months. Recency is a proxy for momentum. Without it, there is nothing to invest behind.

## Example 3

**Company:**
EcoChain Platform (fictional)

**Why rejected:**
Sustainability reporting and ESG compliance platform for "European manufacturers." Strong pitch deck, credible-sounding team, and good media coverage in EU-Startups and Sifted. Problem: the space is extremely crowded (50+ players globally), the fund has no specific advantage in ESG compliance introductions, and the company's primary differentiation is "AI-powered reporting" — a feature, not a product. Already widely known. Zero sourcing edge.

**Red flag:**
Strong media coverage + crowded category + fund has no specific value-add = bad deal even if the company is interesting to others.

**Pattern to avoid:**
Companies in heavily covered categories where the fund has no sourcing or value-add advantage. Being a good company is not enough — the fund must be able to win the deal and add meaningful value. If the company is already on three VC blogs and covered in Sifted, the sourcing edge is gone and the fund will not win at favorable terms.

## Common Bad-Fit Patterns

- Horizontal AI platforms that claim to serve multiple industrial verticals with no specific workflow
- Companies older than 4 years with no recent signal — assume stalled unless proven otherwise
- Heavily media-covered companies in the fund's geography — sourcing edge already gone
- Consumer or prosumer tools reframed as "enterprise" without a real enterprise buyer
- Teams with no prior industrial operations experience claiming deep domain knowledge
- ESG/sustainability reporting tools where the fund has no value-add network
- US-focused companies with no Baltic or Nordic go-to-market path
